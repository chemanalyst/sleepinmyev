How Much Range Do Camping Mattresses Actually Cost?

You're shopping for a camping mattress. You find a 25-pounder you love. Then you wonder: "How much range will this kill?" The internet gives you wildly different answers. We went to the data — here's the honest answer.

By EV Mattress Guide · Updated July 2026 · Sources: EPA testing, Tesla efficiency data, real-world EV owner measurements

The Formula: Industry Standard

The established benchmark in EV efficiency is straightforward: every 100 pounds of additional weight reduces range by approximately 1%. This comes from EPA testing, Tesla's own published data, and thousands of real-world EV owner measurements. It accounts for rolling resistance and the minor aerodynamic effect of cargo inside the vehicle.

The formula: % range loss = (weight in lbs ÷ 100) × 1.0
Example: 25 lb mattress → 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25% range loss

That's it. No complex physics — just a reliable rule of thumb backed by real-world data.

Real Mattress, Real Numbers

Let's put a 25 lb self-inflating mattress — a common mid-range weight — into a Tesla Model Y with 260 miles of EPA-rated range:

What Actually Kills Your Range (Ranked)

If you want to make smart decisions about range, here's where the real losses are:

Cold weather: −15 to −20% range. Winter camping in sub-freezing temps costs you 40–50× more range than any mattress choice. A warmer mattress (high R-value) actually helps by reducing how hard your HVAC works overnight.
Driving at 80 mph: −20 to −30% range. Speed is your single biggest range lever. Slowing from 80 to 65 mph on the highway recovers more range than swapping a 30 lb mattress for a 5 lb one.
Roof box aerodynamic drag: −10 to −15% range. The shape of a roof box at highway speed matters far more than its weight. This is unavoidable for campers who use one — accept the trade-off and plan charging accordingly.
Mattress weight: −0.04 to −0.14% range. The difference between the lightest mattress on our list (3.6 lbs, NEMO Quasar) and the heaviest (14 lbs, Luno AIR+FOAM Pro) is 0.10% range — about 0.26 miles on a 260-mile EV.

The Camp Mode Factor — Unique to EV Camping

Here's something traditional campers don't have: climate control running on battery all night. Tesla Camp Mode, Rivian Camp Mode, and VW ID.Buzz electric climate systems maintain a set temperature overnight on battery — safely, quietly, with no engine running.

This fundamentally changes which mattress you need:

Camping typeR-value neededWhy
Traditional tent (no heat source)R-4 to R-5Mattress must insulate you from cold ground
EV camping with Camp ModeR-1.5 to R-3HVAC maintains temperature; mattress just needs to be comfortable

A lighter, lower R-value mattress with Camp Mode often delivers a warmer, more comfortable night than a heavy R-8 pad in a traditional tent. You're choosing for comfort and fit — not insulation.

The exception: high R-value mattresses actually save battery when Camp Mode is running. If your HVAC has to work less because your mattress insulates well, overnight battery drain is lower. For cold-weather EV camping, a mattress like the Luno AIR+FOAM Pro (R-11.2) or Exped MegaMat Duo (R-8.1) is a genuine efficiency tool, not just a comfort upgrade.

The Bottom Line

A 25 lb camping mattress costs you approximately 0.65 miles of range on a 260-mile EV.

Your roof box, your driving speed, and cold weather each matter 50–200× more than your mattress weight. Pick the mattress that fits your EV, feels comfortable, and suits your budget. Plan charging stops based on trip distance — not mattress weight.
